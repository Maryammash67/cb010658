WILD LIFE

Heritage sri Lanka

Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, and Digamadulla are just a few of the world's ancient cities found in Sri Lanka, which has a history of about three thousand years. In the midst of the towering mountains, remnants of these formerly magnificent towns, with its palaces, temples, monasteries, hospitals, and theatres, lay abandoned. The Kingdom of Anuradhapura is the most renowned and beautiful of all the historical locations. One of the oldest continuously inhabited towns in the world and Sri Lanka's third and longest-serving capital is also one of the most revered by Buddhists worldwide.

In the ninth century BC, Polonnaruwa, the second-oldest kingdom in Sri Lanka, became the nation's political hub for the first time. The Polonnaruwa kingdom built irrigation systems that are far superior to those of the Anuradhapura Age and still supply irrigation water to farmers in and around Polonnaruwa. However, the kingdom's glory was fleeting, and by the eleventh century, an invasion from South India had moved the political centre closer to the middle of the nation. A testament to the discipline and magnificence of the Kingdom's earliest rulers, the ancient city of Polonnaruwa is still one of the best planned archaeological relic sites in the nation today.

During the Anuradhapura era, Digamadulla, the Eastern kingdom of Sri Lanka, served as the nation's agricultural and spiritual centre. Digamadulla, the province under the viceroy of the Anuradhapura king, was a rich region with great palaces, monasteries, and temples at every crossroads. Many of the great works of ancient Digamadulla have been lost to time, conflict, and looting, while many more are silently waiting in the middle of the forests covering the regions of Eastern Sri Lanka. Digamadulla has received less attention from archaeologists than Anuradhapura and Polonnaruwa.
